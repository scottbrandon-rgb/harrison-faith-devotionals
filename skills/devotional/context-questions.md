# Context Questions — Devotional Intake

This file defines the intake conversation that runs before any devotional is written.
Every section is required. Do not skip or combine steps.

---

## Section A — Always-Ask Questions

Ask these every week, in this order, as a clean numbered list. Wait for all answers
before proceeding.

```
Before I write this week's devotionals, I need a few things from you:

1. **Sermon series name** — What is the overarching series title?
   (e.g., "Trust & Obey", "Leaving the 99")

2. **Week title** — What is this specific week called?
   (e.g., "Step and See", "The Risk of Success")

3. **Primary scripture passage** — What is the anchor text for the week?
   Include the full reference and translation (e.g., Joshua 3:13–17 ESV).

4. **The big idea** — In one sentence, what is the central truth you want
   every reader to walk away with this week?

5. **Memory verse** — What is the memory verse for this week?
   Provide the full text and reference (e.g., Proverbs 3:5-6 ESV).

6. **Publish start date** — What Monday does Day 1 publish?
   (e.g., May 11, 2026)

7. **Sermon movements** — List the 5 main movements or points of the sermon
   in order. These become the spine of Days 1–5. A phrase or sentence each is enough.
   (e.g., "1. The flood stage sets the scene / 2. God's presence is the proof /
   3. Step before you see / 4. God is already at Adam / 5. Keep your feet in the water")

8. **Historical and word study details** — List any cultural background,
   Hebrew/Greek word studies, or historical context from the sermon that must
   be woven into the devotionals. Include the day you want each detail to land on,
   if you have a preference.
```

---

## Section B — Conditional Questions

Ask only when the source manuscript triggers one of these conditions.

**Multiple anchor passages:**
If the manuscript draws from more than one primary text across the week, ask:
> "Which passage anchors each day? Walk me through the text assignment for Days 1–5."

**Series continuity:**
If this is not the first week of the series, ask:
> "What did last week cover? Is there a thread, image, or idea from last week
> that should carry into this week's opening?"

**Unresolved big idea:**
If the answer to question 4 is vague or contains more than one idea, ask:
> "If you could say only one thing to your congregation this week, what would it be?"

---

## Section C — Confirmation Summary

After collecting all answers, feed back a summary in this exact format and
wait for explicit confirmation before writing a single word of devotional content.

```
Here is what I understand about this week before I begin:

- Series: [sermon series name]
- Week title: [week title]
- Anchor text: [passage + translation]
- Big idea: [one sentence]
- Memory verse: [reference + full text]
- Publish dates: [Monday] through [Friday]
- The 5 movements:
    Day 1 — [movement 1]
    Day 2 — [movement 2]
    Day 3 — [movement 3]
    Day 4 — [movement 4]
    Day 5 — [movement 5]
- Historical/word study notes: [list, or "none provided"]

Is this correct? Or do you want to adjust anything before I begin writing?
```

Do not proceed until the user responds with confirmation (e.g., "yes", "correct",
"go ahead") or makes corrections. If corrections are made, update the summary
and confirm again.

---

## Section D — Per-Day Variation

Each day covers one movement of the sermon. Within that constraint, each day
should have a distinct posture so the five days do not read as repetition.

**Day 1 — Entry point**
Set the scene. Introduce the passage and its world. Give the reader just enough
context to feel the weight of what's coming. Pull from Movement 1 of the sermon.
This day earns the reader's trust. It does not resolve anything.

**Day 2 — Theological depth**
Go into the text. Unpack the doctrine or truth at the heart of Movement 2.
This is the most theologically rich day of the week. Define the key terms.
Show what the passage is actually claiming.

**Day 3 — Historical and cultural anchor**
This is the word study / background day. Use the historical, cultural, or Hebrew/Greek
detail provided in the intake to open up the text in a way the reader would not
find on their own. Anchor this in Movement 3. Make the ancient world feel real.

**Day 4 — Application**
Bring it close. This is the most personal day of the week. Draw from Movement 4
and bring the truth down to ground level. What does this look like on a Tuesday?
In a marriage? In a bank account? In a conversation someone is avoiding?

**Day 5 — Doxological close**
End the week in awe. Movement 5 should land the reader in worship, not just
resolve the week's argument. Leave them with something larger than a takeaway —
leave them with God. This day is the lightest on instruction and the heaviest
on wonder.

## Application Questions — Structure (Every Day)

Each day ends with exactly two application questions:

1. **Introspective question** — Turns the reader inward.
   Begins with phrases like: "Where in your life...", "When have you...",
   "What is it in you that...", "Honestly, do you..."

2. **Action-oriented question** — Turns the reader outward or forward.
   Begins with phrases like: "What would it look like to...",
   "Who is one person you could...", "What is one step you could take...",
   "What would change if you actually..."

Questions should be plainly worded. No theological jargon. One sentence each.
They should feel like a conversation, not a worksheet.
