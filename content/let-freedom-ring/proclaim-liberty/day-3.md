## Devotional

In September of 1620, a pastor named John Robinson watched 102 people from his congregation board a ship called the Mayflower. They were separatists, believers who looked at the corruption in the church of England and decided the ship was already burning. They aimed for Jamestown, where the king's rule ran thin. They missed it by 500 miles and landed at Plymouth instead, staring at a wilderness with a problem nobody in their world had faced. No king had appointed anyone to take charge. Who governs when no crown says so?

Their answer changed history. They gave themselves a government. The Mayflower Compact was written by ordinary church members who believed authority could rise from a covenanted people, and the idea did not come from a philosopher. It came from Scripture.

Jesus said, I will build my church, and the gates of hell shall not prevail against it (Matthew 16:18). The word He chose was ekklesia, the called out assembly, a people whose authority comes from somewhere higher than this world. These congregations had practiced that authority for years. They chose their own elders. They brought discipline before the whole assembly, the way Jesus taught (Matthew 18:17). They followed the pattern God gave Israel: look for able men who fear God, men who are trustworthy, and place them over the people (Exodus 18:21). Choose for your tribes wise, understanding, and experienced men (Deuteronomy 1:13). When the moment came to build a public government, the church simply did in the open what it had been doing in the pews.

Israel's government had worked the same way, and one picture captures it. Rather than a pyramid where the king's will rolled downhill, Israel grew like a living tree, from the bottom up, every cell contributing to the growth of the whole. Leaders rose from anywhere. Gideon came from an obscure family. Deborah was an honest woman who knew the Law. The tree drew life from every root.

Sixteen years after Plymouth, a pastor named Thomas Hooker was pushed out of Massachusetts for believing every Christian should vote, so he walked his whole congregation through the wilderness to found Hartford. When they asked him how to set up their government, he preached Deuteronomy 1:13 and declared that the foundation of authority is laid in the free consent of the people. From that sermon came the Fundamental Orders of Connecticut, and from that blueprint, in time, came three words you know: We the people.

So how you carry yourself inside the body of Christ is never a private matter. The way you submit, serve, vote, forgive, and follow in the church becomes the way you shape everything outside it. Those 102 people thought they were simply being faithful. They were planting a nation.

A government of the people carries one hidden requirement, though, and the second president of the United States named it out loud. Tomorrow we look at what John Adams knew our Constitution could not survive without.

## Application

1. If your conduct inside the church became the pattern for a whole community, what kind of community would grow from it?
2. What is one responsibility in the body of Christ you have been leaving to others, and what step will you take toward it this month?

## Challenge

Find one place this week to practice being the ekklesia. Serve where nobody appointed you, encourage a leader you usually critique, or pick up a responsibility you have quietly avoided. The church taught a nation how to govern by doing what Scripture said. Do what Scripture says and let God decide what grows from it.

## Prayer

Father, You called me out of this world and into an assembly that answers to You. Thank You that the gates of hell cannot prevail against what Jesus is building. Teach me to carry my part of the body with faithfulness, to serve without being appointed and to submit without being forced. Make my private obedience the kind of root You can grow something public from. Build Your church, Lord, and build it in me first. Amen.
