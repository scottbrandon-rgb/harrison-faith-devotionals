## Devotional

My great, great, great grandfather knew what it was like to live without freedom. Four generations. That is all that separates me from a man who could only hope that liberty was possible, and I have spent most of my life treating his hope like my birthright. That is the danger of a gift. Hold it long enough and you forget somebody handed it to you.

God built a reminder into Israel's calendar so His people could never forget. Every fiftieth year the trumpet sounded and liberty was proclaimed throughout the land to all its inhabitants (Leviticus 25:10). Debts were released. Servants went home. Land returned to the families who had lost it. Here is what should stop us cold: nobody in Israel earned the fiftieth year. The poor man could never buy his land back. The servant could never purchase his own release. Liberty was proclaimed. Liberty was announced. Liberty was received. The whole year rested on the character of the God who commanded it, and the only thing a man could do with jubilee was walk home grateful.

Our nation's freedom carries the same fingerprint. We sing let freedom ring, and we understand the sentiment, though I wonder if we understand the sacrifice. More than 50,000 lives were given in the wars and hardships that established this country. Men bled for a liberty they were unsure of but willing to try at all costs. You and I woke up this morning inside the answer to their hope, and most mornings we never think about it once.

Gratitude is the first act of stewardship. Before you defend a gift, before you use a gift, before you pass a gift along, you thank the giver. Jesus said that if the Son sets you free, you will be free indeed (John 8:36), and that freedom follows the pattern of jubilee exactly. It was proclaimed by another, purchased by another, and received by you with empty hands.

When freedom is received rightly, it produces a people who remember where it came from. Israel remembered so well that for centuries they did something no other nation on earth dared to do. They lived without a king, and the story of their government became the seed of ours. That anomaly is where we walk tomorrow.

Four generations back, a man hoped. Today I am free. The distance between those two sentences is grace, and grace deserves thanks before it deserves anything else.

## Application

1. Which of your freedoms, in your country or in Christ, have you been treating like a birthright rather than a gift?
2. Who in your life paid a price for something you now enjoy, and how will you thank them this week?

## Challenge

Before the day ends, write down three freedoms you did not earn. One from your country, one from your family, one from your salvation. Then thank God for each one out loud, by name. Gratitude that stays in your head never becomes stewardship.

## Prayer

Father, You proclaimed a liberty over me that I could never have purchased for myself. Forgive me for the mornings I wake up in freedom and never look up. Teach me to hold this gift with open and grateful hands, to remember the ones who bled for it, and to remember the Son who bled for something greater. Make me a steward of what I received, and never let me confuse a gift with a wage. Amen.
