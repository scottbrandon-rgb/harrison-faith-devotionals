## Devotional

When I was a kid it seemed like I was always in trouble, and half the time it was for things I did not do. There was plenty I deserved, don't misunderstand me. But somewhere in those early years I picked up a way of thinking that said, if something went wrong, I must have caused it, even when I couldn't remember doing it. Funny how that thinking follows you into your walk with God. Since I know God is never in the wrong, I just assume that if something is off between us, the fault is mine. So when I hit empty, my first move is an audit, hunting through my life for the failure that explains it. I mean, it couldn't be God so it had to be me.

Maybe you run the same kind of "audit." So, when the tank hits empty, what do you say? 
I failed. 
I must have missed God. 
He's done with me. 
The enemy loves that math, because he has convinced a whole generation of believers that emptiness is evidence. Evidence you blew it. Evidence you're finished. Evidence God has moved on to somebody more useful. Last week I told you the tank can run dry even when you did everything right. This week I want to show you why God lets that happen.

Start with the emptiest man who ever lived. Paul says Jesus, existing in the very form of God, "emptied himself" (Philippians 2:7). The Greek word is ekenosen, from kenoo, to pour out until nothing remains. Read that verse slowly and notice who did the emptying. Nobody drained Him, nobody caught Him off guard. He emptied Himself, and He did it in obedience, "obedient to the point of death, even death on a cross" (Philippians 2:8). Now follow the logic all the way down. If emptiness always meant failure, then the cross was a failure. If an empty tank always meant a wrong turn, then Jesus took a wrong turn. But you and I know better. The emptiness of Christ was the will of the Father being carried out in the Son, which means emptiness, all by itself, proves nothing about failure. Let me say that a bit clearly. Emptiness is not the clear indicator of failure.

Now, somebody is already asking, doesn't God discipline His children? Couldn't my empty season be correction? Yes, He does, and it could be. Some emptiness follows disobedience, ask Jonah. But even then, Scripture says He "disciplines us for our good, that we may share his holiness," and that the pain "yields the peaceful fruit of righteousness" (Hebrews 12:10-11). God never empties His children out of spite. Whether your season is shaping you or sending you, the character of God stays the same.

So here is foundational sentence I'm going to build the entire week of devotionals on. 

**Emptiness coupled with obedience is the Father's will being fulfilled in you.**

## Application

1. When you hit empty, what verdict do you hand yourself, and where did you learn to read exhaustion as God's rejection?
2. What would change today if you traded the question "What did I do wrong?" for "Where was my obedience when this emptying started?"

## Challenge

Run the better audit today. Sit down with the Lord, name your empty season plainly, and answer one question in writing: where was my obedience when the pouring started? Keep that answer where you can find it, because you will need it every day this week.

## Prayer

Father, I have spent years reading my empty seasons as Your verdict against me, auditing my life for a failure You never charged me with. Thank You that Your Son emptied Himself on purpose, in obedience, and that His empty tank was Your will being done and not a wrong turn. Teach me this week to ask the better question, not what did I do wrong, but where was my obedience when the pouring began. Silence the accuser who keeps calling my emptiness evidence. In Jesus' name, amen.
