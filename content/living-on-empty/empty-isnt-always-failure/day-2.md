## Devotional

Paul is sitting in a prison cell when he writes Philippians, and when he reaches for a picture of his own suffering, look at the one he chooses. "Even if I am to be poured out as a drink offering upon the sacrificial offering of your faith, I am glad" (Philippians 2:17). That is an Old Testament picture, and you need to see it the way his first readers did. When Israel brought a sacrifice, the law also called for a drink offering, wine poured out beside the altar (Numbers 15). And here is what set the drink offering apart from nearly every other offering in the system. Nothing came back. With the grain offering, the priest kept a portion. With the peace offering, the worshiper ate a portion. The drink offering was poured out to the last drop, and the ground took all of it.

Now trace the pattern through Scripture, because this image did not start with Paul and it does not end with him. In verse 7, Christ is poured out. Isaiah saw it seven hundred years early, "he poured out his soul to death" (Isaiah 53:12). Paul says it here from prison, and then says it again at the very end of his life, "I am already being poured out as a drink offering" (2 Timothy 4:6). The pattern runs from Christ, to His servant, to us. This is the family trade, church. This is what the household of God does. We get poured out.

So let me give you two definitions, and I want you to hold them together for the rest of the week. Suffering is the pouring. Emptiness is the emptied cup. They are one motion viewed from two angles. The pouring is what it feels like while it is happening, and the empty cup is what you are holding when it is done. When you say "I'm suffering," you are describing the tilt of the cup. When you say "I'm empty," you are describing what is left in it. Same offering. Same altar. Same God doing the pouring.

And that last phrase is where the fear rises up, isn't it? Because if God is the One tilting the cup, then the question every empty believer asks next is exactly the right question. Why? Why would God pour me out, and what is He doing with what He drains? We will spend the rest of this week answering that, and Scripture gives three answers with three faces on them. Jesus shows us qualification. Joseph shows us maturation. Elijah shows us transition.

But before any of that, settle today's truth in your soul. Your emptiness has an address, and the address is the altar. Wine on the ground next to the altar was never a tragedy, it was worship. The difference between a spill and an offering is the altar it lands beside.

**The difference between a spill and an offering is the altar.**

## Application

1. When you say "I'm empty," have you been describing an accident or an offering, and which one do you actually believe it is?
2. What is one drained place in your life you have been calling a spill that your obedience says was poured out beside the altar?

## Challenge

Every time you catch yourself saying "I'm exhausted" or "I'm empty" today, finish the sentence. Name where the pouring went, the people it served, the obedience it funded, the assignment it carried. An offering deserves to be named as one, so stop filing your worship under accidents.

## Prayer

Lord, I have looked at my empty cup and called it a tragedy, when You were receiving it as a drink offering the whole time. Thank You that I stand in a long line, from Your Son, to Paul, to every saint who ever got poured out, and that the family trade is not wasted on the ground. Help me hold both truths at once, that the pouring hurts and the pouring is worship. Let me trust the hand that tilts the cup. In Jesus' name, amen.
