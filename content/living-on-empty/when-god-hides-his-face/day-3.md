## Devotional

Everything in this psalm pivots on one small word, and if you blink you will miss it. Look at verse 5. "But I have trusted in your steadfast love" (Psalm 13:5). That word, but, is the hinge the whole psalm swings on. Nothing has changed between verse 4 and verse 5. The enemies are still circling and the answer still hasn't come. God still hasn't said a word. The only thing that changed is David, because somewhere in the praying his eyes came open, and a man who can finally see is a man who can say but.

Notice where David plants his weight. Not on God's power. Not on God's justice. He plants it on God's "steadfast love," and behind those two English words sits one Hebrew word, hesed. Hesed is covenant love, the loyal love bound by God's own promise and God's own character, the love that keeps its word when you have run clean out of reasons to deserve it. David does not reach for God's justice, because David is not sure his own life could survive it. He reaches instead for the one thing about God that never depended on David at all. When you cannot trust what you have done, you can still trust who He is.

Jeremiah learned the same thing sitting in the rubble of a burned-down Jerusalem, and he wrote it down. "The steadfast love of the Lord never ceases; his mercies never come to an end; they are new every morning; great is your faithfulness" (Lamentations 3:22-23). New every morning. That means the mercy did not run out on your worst morning, and it will not run out on tomorrow's. His promises are rooted in His character, and His character does not move when your circumstances do. Just as surely as the sun comes up, so does His mercy, whether or not you feel the warmth of it yet.

Now watch David do something most of us never think to do. He preaches to his own heart. "My heart shall rejoice," he says, and that word shall is a command, not a feeling. He stops letting his feelings run him and starts telling them where to go. And look at the ground he stands on to do it. "I will sing to the Lord, because he has dealt bountifully with me." He has dealt. That is past tense. David is still empty in the present and still lamenting in the present, so he reaches back and sings from the past. He counts what God has already done and lets that bounty carry him through what God has not done yet.

This is the muscle nobody wants to build, because it only ever gets built in the dark. Learning to sing from His past faithfulness when you cannot see His present hand. So when the night runs long, stop interviewing your feelings and start preaching to them. Tell your heart what is still true. Count it out loud if you have to, one mercy at a time, and let the God who has already dealt bountifully with you be enough to sing about before He is anything you can see.

**Sing from His past faithfulness when you cannot see His present hand.**

## Application

1. Whose voice has been running your heart lately, your feelings or the Word, and which one have you actually been believing?
2. What is one thing God has already done for you that you could count out loud today and sing from, even though nothing in front of you has changed?

## Challenge

Make a list today of what God has already done. Not what you are waiting on Him to do, what He has already, actually done, starting at the cross and working forward. When your feelings start preaching their sermon tonight, read your list back to them out loud. Sing from the past until the present catches up.

## Prayer

Lord, my feelings have been preaching to me all season, and I have believed them over Your Word. Teach me to do what David did, to plant my whole weight on Your hesed, the love that holds because You promised and not because I earned it. Remind me what You have already done when I cannot see what You are doing now. And let Your past faithfulness be enough to put a song in my mouth before the morning ever comes. In Jesus' name, amen.
