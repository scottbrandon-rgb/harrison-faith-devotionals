## Devotional

Maybe you have prayed the ugliest version of David's prayer. Not just how long, but where were You. When the diagnosis came back. When the marriage finally cracked in half. When you buried someone you had no business burying yet, and heaven felt like a ceiling over your prayers. I am not going to tell you that feeling is faithless, because the most faithful man who ever lived prayed it first, and He prayed it from a cross.

When Jesus was dying in Matthew 27, He did not reach for a triumphant line. He reached for a lament. He prayed the opening words of Psalm 22, "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" (Matthew 27:46). The Son of God, hanging in the dark, asking His Father the very question you have been too afraid to say out loud. Sit there a second before you rush ahead to Sunday. God the Son knew, in His own body, what it feels like when the Father's face goes dark.

Here is what was happening inside that darkness, and it is the hinge everything hangs on. On that cross the Father turned His face away from the Son so that He would never once have to turn it away from you. Jesus took the true hiding, the real forsakenness, the abandonment you and I actually earned, and He drank it to the bottom. Every empty morning you have ever dreaded, every fear that God has finally walked out on you, He met at Golgotha and absorbed into Himself. What you feel in your worst season, He endured in fact, so that you would never have to endure it in truth.

That is why David and you are not standing in the same spot. David sang, "my heart shall rejoice in your salvation," and he was singing about a rescue he only held as a promise, a thing he trusted God to pull off someday. You and I live on the other side of that someday. We are not trusting a promise God might yet keep, but a work He has already finished and a tomb He has already emptied. If David could sing from a promise he had never seen, how much more can you sing from a grave that is already standing open.

So when the season goes black and you can count on nothing else, count the cross. Whatever you cannot feel about God's nearness today, you can still know exactly what His nearness cost Him. He was forsaken so that the word forsaken could be struck out of your future for good, because this is the God who said "I will never leave you nor forsake you" (Hebrews 13:5), and He signed that promise in His own blood. Your feelings say He is gone. The cross says He would rather die than lose you. One of them is lying, and it isn't the cross.

**The Father hid His face from the Son so it would never be hidden from you.**

## Application

1. What is the where-were-You question you have been afraid to pray, the one you assumed a real Christian is not allowed to ask?
2. When the feeling that God is gone comes back today, what would it look like to answer it with the cross instead of arguing with the feeling?

## Challenge

Today, preach the cross to yourself before your feelings preach anything to you. Take the one lie you keep believing in the dark, that God has left, that He stopped caring, that He forgot your name, and hold it up against Matthew 27:46. Then say it plainly. He was forsaken so I never will be. Let the fact outrank the feeling for one whole day.

## Prayer

Jesus, You prayed my ugliest prayer before I ever could, and You prayed it from a cross. Thank You that the Father turned His face from You so He would never turn it from me, that You drank the forsakenness I earned all the way to the bottom. When I feel abandoned this week, remind me it is a feeling and not a fact, because You made certain of that at Golgotha. If I can count on nothing else in the dark, let me count the cross. In Your name, amen.
