## Devotional

There's a detail in this verse that you cannot afford to miss. When the shepherd finds the lost sheep, he does not point it toward home and say, "There you go. You know the way." He does not put a leash on it and drag it back. He does not scold it, reject it, or give it one good lecture about how this better not happen again. No. He lays it on his shoulders. He carries it. And the sheep contributes absolutely nothing to its own rescue except its weight. That right there is the gospel.

See, there's a condition that shepherds know about called a "cast down" sheep. It's what happens when a sheep lies down in a spot that's just a little too comfortable, maybe a soft divot in the ground, and it rolls onto its back. Once that happens, the sheep cannot get itself upright again. Gases build in its stomach, blood flow gets cut off from its legs, and the longer it stays down, the less capable it becomes of ever standing back up. And I think that's a picture of a lot of us. We found the comfortable spot, the easy place, the thing that felt good for a while, and now we're stuck. We keep saying "I'm fine, I've got this, just give me a minute," kicking our legs and getting nowhere. We feel like one more sermon, one more good week, one more answered prayer will be enough to get us back on our feet. But the truth is, the longer you're down, the less capable you are of getting yourself up.

That's exactly why the shepherd doesn't ask you to walk yourself home. He knows you can't. Ephesians 2 says you were dead in your trespasses and sins. Not weak, not struggling, not almost there. Dead. And dead things do not get up on their own. But God, being rich in mercy because of the great love with which He loved us, even when we were dead, made us alive together with Christ. The shepherd reached down, picked up seventy-five pounds of dead weight, and placed it on His own shoulders. He bore your disobedience, your stubbornness, your sin, your generational mess, your baggage, all of it. And He carried it Himself because He knew you never could. The Philippians 2 hymn says He did not count equality with God a thing to be grasped, but emptied Himself, taking the form of a servant, becoming obedient to the point of death. He came down from glory so He could bend low enough to lift you up.

So what I'm seeing is this: you didn't initiate your salvation, you didn't coordinate your salvation, and you don't sustain your salvation. The shepherd came, the shepherd found, the shepherd carries. That's who He is. And the only thing you bring to the table is the very thing He came to redeem. Tomorrow we're going to see what was in the shepherd's heart as he made that long walk home... and it wasn't resentment. It was joy.

## Application

1. What does it reveal about your view of God if you keep trying to earn your way back into His good graces rather than allowing Him to carry you?

2. Where in your life are you still "kicking your legs," trying to get up on your own, when the shepherd is standing right there ready to lift you?

## Challenge

Today, take one thing you have been carrying on your own, a sin, a shame, a weight that keeps pressing you down, and physically write it on a piece of paper. Then fold it up, set it at the foot of a cross or a Bible, wherever you pray, and leave it there. Say out loud: "This is Yours to carry, not mine." Don't pick it back up.

## Prayer

Lord, I confess that I have been trying to walk myself home. I have been kicking and striving and telling You I've got it handled when the truth is I have been stuck. I cannot get up on my own. I cannot save myself. Today I stop pretending that I can. Thank You for not waiting for me to figure it out, for bending down and lifting me up, for putting the full weight of my sin and my mess on Your own shoulders. You bore what I could never bear. Help me to stop struggling against Your rescue and just let You carry me. In Jesus' name, amen.
