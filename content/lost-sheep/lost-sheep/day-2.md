## Devotional

There's something about sheep that most people don't realize, and it's this: a sheep does not know when it is lost. The moment it wanders beyond familiar territory, it's completely disoriented, but it doesn't feel disoriented. It just keeps grazing. It keeps walking. It keeps doing what feels normal because that's the nature of a sheep. It normalizes its lostness. And if you've ever watched a sheep wander, you know they don't move in straight lines. They move in circles, following their nose from one patch of grass to the next, and the whole time they feel like they're going somewhere when really they're just spinning in the same patterns. Sound familiar?

Isaiah says it plainly. All we like sheep have gone astray, every one of us to his own way. Now watch this... the verse says "every one to his own way," which means my path away from the Lord probably looks different from yours. Your wandering might be a relationship you won't let go of. Mine might be an ambition I put above everything else. Somebody else's might be a comfort they've made into a god. The paths are different, but they all lead the same direction, and that direction is away from the shepherd. And here is what makes it so dangerous: because the paths are all different, we can look at someone else's wandering and think, "well, at least I'm not doing that," while we're just as lost on our own road. Romans 3:10-12 says it bluntly: none is righteous, no not one. No one understands. No one seeks for God. All have turned aside.

So what does a lost sheep do when it doesn't know it's lost? It doesn't cry out. It doesn't call for help. It doesn't even look for the shepherd. It just keeps living as if everything is fine because its condition feels normal. And I think that's where a lot of us live. We've got routines and rhythms and blessings and enough comfort to convince us that we're okay. The bills are mostly paid. The marriage is mostly working. The kids mostly respect us. And we say "I'm doing pretty good," because compared to whoever we're comparing ourselves to, we look fine. But the shepherd is counting, and He knows you're not in the fold. Your comfort doesn't mean you are safe, it just means you can't feel the danger.

That's the real trap of lostness. It doesn't feel like a crisis. It feels like a Tuesday. And because it feels normal, you don't ask for help, you don't seek a rescuer, you don't even think you need one. But the shepherd sees what you can't assess about your own heart, your own marriage, your own soul. And what we're going to see tomorrow is that not only do you not know you're lost... you don't have the resources to save yourself even if you did.

## Application

1. What area of your life have you been calling "normal" or "fine" that might actually be evidence of distance from the shepherd?

2. Who or what have you been comparing yourself to in order to feel okay about where you are spiritually?

## Challenge

Write down one area of your life where you have been going through the motions and calling it enough. Be honest about it. Then bring it before the Lord today and say, "I don't want to normalize this anymore." Don't try to fix it yet. Just name it.

## Prayer

Lord, I confess that I have been calling things normal that are not what You designed for me. I have drifted and dressed it up as routine. I have wandered and called it living. Open my eyes today to see what I have been unable to see about my own condition. I don't want to keep walking in circles and calling it progress. Show me where I am, not where I think I am. And give me the courage to stop pretending that comfortable means close to You. In Jesus' name, amen.
