Title
The Destination Is Joy

Excerpt
"If the destination of each parable is joy, then maybe joy is not just a byproduct of the kingdom. Maybe joy is the passion that fuels something bigger."

Scripture Reading
Luke 15:7 ESV
"Just so, I tell you, there will be more joy in heaven over one sinner who repents than over ninety-nine righteous persons who need no repentance."

Devotional Thought

If I asked you to name the components of the kingdom of God, you would probably land on righteousness and peace pretty quickly. That makes sense. If righteousness increases, so does the kingdom. If peace increases, so does the kingdom. But Paul throws something into Romans 14:17 that might not feel as obvious... joy. "For the kingdom of God is not a matter of eating and drinking but of righteousness and peace and joy in the Holy Spirit."

Joy. Not happiness, not comfort, not good vibes on a Sunday morning, but joy as a kingdom component right alongside righteousness and peace.

Here's what I see. We tend to treat joy like a nice side effect of following Jesus. Something we experience when things go well or when worship hits just right. But Paul doesn't list it as a bonus feature. He puts it in the same sentence as the stuff we already know builds the kingdom. So if righteousness expanding means the kingdom is expanding, and peace expanding means the kingdom is expanding, then it sounds a little different (but just as true) to say that when joy increases, when it multiplies, so does the kingdom.

Now watch this. When you read Luke 15, you find three parables back to back. A lost sheep, a lost coin, a lost son. And there is a pattern running through each one of them that you might skip right past if you're not looking for it. It goes like this... lost, sought, found, restored, and celebrated. Every single time. Three stories, three different things lost, but the same destination in all of them. Celebration. Joy.

The shepherd finds the sheep and calls his friends to celebrate. The woman finds her coin and calls her neighbors to celebrate. The father sees his son and throws a party. And Jesus makes the point unmistakable when He says there is more joy in heaven over one sinner who repents than over ninety nine who need no repentance.

So the goal of each parable is not just that the lost gets found. The goal is that we arrive at the same destination that heaven does. Joy. Which means joy is not just a feeling you get after the hard work is done, but rather a driving force behind the work itself. Joy is not the reward at the end of the kingdom... it is the fuel that moves the kingdom forward.

And I think that reframes something for us. Because most of us are not short on obligation when it comes to reaching the lost. We know we should. We know the Great Commission. We know the scriptures. But obligation without passion runs out of gas eventually. What keeps a shepherd walking through the dark looking for one sheep that wandered off? It is not duty alone. It is the joy of bringing that sheep home.

If joy is the destination of every parable in Luke 15, then maybe it is time we stop treating it like a nice feeling and start treating it like the kingdom passion it actually is.

Tomorrow we are going to look at what makes heaven itself shout for joy, and the answer might surprise you.

Application Questions
1. When you think about joy in your faith, do you tend to treat it as a nice benefit of following Jesus or as something that actually drives and expands the kingdom?
2. In the pattern of lost, sought, found, restored, and celebrated, which part do you most naturally connect with and which part feels most unfamiliar in your own life?

Today's Challenge
Read through all three parables in Luke 15 in one sitting. As you read, circle or underline every reference to joy, celebration, gladness, or rejoicing. Let the repetition speak for itself.

Prayer to Pray
Father, I confess that I have treated joy like something extra, something nice to have when everything else is in order. But Your word says joy is part of the kingdom itself. Teach me what that means. Open my eyes to see that joy is not just a destination I arrive at when things go well, but a passion You have placed in me that moves me toward the things that matter most to You. Begin to stir that joy in me this week. In Jesus' name, amen.
