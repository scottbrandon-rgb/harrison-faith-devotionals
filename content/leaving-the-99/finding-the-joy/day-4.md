## Title
The Comfort of the 99

## Scripture Reading
Luke 15:1-2 ESV
"Now the tax collectors and sinners were all drawing near to hear him. And the Pharisees and the scribes grumbled, saying, 'This man receives sinners and eats with them.'"

## Devotional Thought

There is a detail in Luke 15 that we read right past if we are not careful, and it is actually the whole reason Jesus told these parables in the first place. Tax collectors and sinners were drawing near to hear Him. And the Pharisees and scribes grumbled about it. That is the setup. Jesus did not tell the parable of the lost sheep to encourage the lost. He told it to confront the found.

In first century Judaism there was a group called the Haverim, the associates of scrupulous people. They prided themselves on going beyond what the law required. When the Old Testament said not to boil a young goat in its mother's milk, the Haverim took that and built an entire system around it... don't eat meat and cheese at the same time, and if you do, wait a specified number of hours before eating the other. They wanted to appear pure, holy, righteous. But it was all a show.

And the people who did not keep the law the way they did, who did not tithe the way they did, who did not perform the way they did? The Haverim called them the Am Ha'Aretz, the people of the land. Being near them jeopardized their cleanliness.

So what I'm seeing is this. The Haverim did not hate the lost. That is the part that should make you uncomfortable. They were not cruel people in the obvious sense. They just loved their comfort more than they loved the one who was missing. They loved their routines, their traditions, their gatherings that affirmed what they already believed. They did not mind giving to the lost, in that they were generous from a distance, but they did not have the patience for the messiness that comes with actually being near someone who is far from God.

And here is where it gets personal. In Ezekiel 34:4, God accuses the false shepherds of Israel with these words... "The strayed you have not brought back, the lost you have not sought." That is a devastating charge. Not because they actively drove the lost away, but because they simply did not go looking. They used their position to justify why they could not care.

Now look at Luke 15:1 again. Tax collectors and sinners drew near to Jesus. They were drawn to the true Shepherd. It was the Pharisees and the scribes who pushed them away. And that should make every one of us stop and examine ourselves. Do the lost see the Shepherd in you? Do they see a way home in you? Do lost sons and daughters see a loving father waiting for them in you? Or do they see the Haverim... someone who is comfortable with the 99 and has no intention of leaving?

Because here is the hard truth. You can love God, attend church, read your Bible, tithe faithfully, and still be a false shepherd if you have made the 99 your permanent home instead of your temporary base.

Tomorrow we will look at what happens when we finally decide to leave, and what the lost are actually looking for when they look at the church.

## Application Questions
1. When you honestly examine your life, do you see more of the Shepherd or more of the Haverim in the way you relate to people who are far from God?
2. Is there a comfort, routine, or circle in your life that has quietly become a reason you do not engage with the lost?

## Today's Challenge
Think of one person in your life who is far from God that you have kept at arm's length, not because you dislike them but because engaging with them would require leaving the comfort of your routine. Pray for them by name today and ask God to give you the willingness to move toward them.

## Prayer to Pray
Father, I do not want to be a Haverim. I do not want to love my comfort more than I love the one who is missing. Search my heart and show me where I have used my routines, my preferences, my religious habits as an excuse to stay put while someone is still lost. Give me the courage to be honest about it and the grace to move toward the people I have been comfortable avoiding. Make me look more like the Shepherd and less like the scribes who grumbled when sinners drew near. In Jesus' name, amen.
