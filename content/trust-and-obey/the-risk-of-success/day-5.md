## Devotional

Four times in Joshua chapter one, the Lord tells Joshua to be strong and courageous. Four times. And the one that carries the most weight is this one. Not because the words are different, but because of what God says right before them. Have I not commanded you?

That question is not rhetorical in the way we usually mean it. It is confrontational. God is not asking because He needs Joshua to confirm it. He is asking because He wants Joshua to hear himself say yes. Because when you know you have been commanded, the question of whether you should go is already settled. The only question left is whether you will.

Here is what I want you to understand about courage. It does not mean you are not afraid. The Hebrew word amats, used here for courageous, means to be firm, to be resolute, to hold your position. It is the picture of a man whose hands are shaking and whose feet are still moving. You do not cower. Not because the thing is not terrifying. Because the presence behind the command will not let you stay on the ground.

Now I want you to think about Joshua at Ai. Ai was the city after the victory at Jericho. It was smaller, easier, and Joshua did not consult God before he went up. He figured the momentum from Jericho would carry him through. He sent a few thousand men, did not bother to ask whether God had commanded him to go, and 36 men came home in caskets. Joshua fell on his face before the Lord: "I thought You would never leave me."

And the Lord said get up.

Not gently. Not with a long speech. Get up. The failure at Ai was not a matter of God abandoning Joshua. It was a matter of Joshua never asking if God had commanded him to go in the first place. That is the entire difference between Jericho and Ai. At Jericho, Joshua obeyed a specific command from God. At Ai, he assumed permission because of the last thing God did.

Some of you are sitting in an Ai right now. You went forward on your own momentum, without stopping to ask if God sent you. And the defeat is real, and the pain is real, and going back feels impossible because the failure is already there waiting for you. Here is what the Lord says to you today. Get up. Repent. Return. Because true failure is not the Ai you walked into. True failure is staying on the ground after God has already told you to rise.

His word never returns void. He still means what He said. He will not leave you, and He will not forsake you, and He is with you wherever you go. The question is the same one He asked Joshua, and He is asking you right now.

Have I not commanded you?

If He has, then what is standing between you and the next step is not the size of the problem or the memory of the last failure. It is your willingness to trust the One who commands and completes.

## Application

1. Look back over the past year. Where did you act on your own momentum, like Joshua at Ai, without first asking if God had commanded you? What happened, and what did you do with it?
2. Is God calling you to get up from a past failure right now? What would repenting and returning actually look like in practical terms this week?

## Challenge

Before you make any significant decision this week, stop and ask yourself one question: Has God commanded this? Not does this seem good, not does this make sense on paper, but has He commanded me. If you cannot clearly say yes, take it to Him in prayer before you take another step. That one pause has saved more people from Ai than any strategy ever has.

## Prayer

Father, I need to be honest with You today. There are places in my life where I went forward without asking if You sent me. I assumed Your presence meant Your permission. Today I am getting up from the ground. I repent of the places where I moved in my own strength and called it faith. I am returning to You. I trust Your word. I know that when You command, You complete. Speak to me today. Name my Jericho. Name my Ai. And give me the courage to respond with undivided obedience. In Jesus' name, amen.
