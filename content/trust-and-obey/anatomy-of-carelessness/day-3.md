## Devotional

I can tell how much I trust God by how quickly I stop asking Him. That is the quiet danger in Joshua 7, and it has a name. Presumption.

Presumption is the child of pride. Pride says, I am enough. Presumption answers, so I can handle this. It is sitting right there in the scouting report. Let about two or three thousand men go up and attack Ai, for they are few (Joshua 7:3). Read it like a battle plan, because that is what it is. The men went up, looked around, came back, and handed Joshua the orders. Send a few thousand. They are small. We have this.

Now ask one question. Who set the strategy at Jericho? God did. Seven days of marching, silence, the ark, the horns, the shout on the last day. Every detail came down from heaven, and Israel followed it to the letter. So who set the strategy at Ai? The men did. Nobody asked the Lord whether to go up, how many to send, or whether Ai was even theirs to take. They measured the battle by the size of the enemy instead of seeking the strength of God.

This is in their bloodline. Go back to Numbers 14. The generation before this one refused to go up when God commanded it. Then the next morning, full of regret and their own will, they decided to charge in after all, even though God had already said no. The text is blunt. "They presumed to go up to the heights of the country, while the ark of the covenant of the LORD and Moses did not depart out of the camp" (Numbers 14:44). The Hebrew there pictures a man swelling up, puffed and heedless, marching off without God. They were beaten back to a place called Hormah.

So the fathers presumed and lost, and now the children line up to do the same thing. Same sin, new address.

There was a man in Israel's future who got this right. David never presumed a battle in his life. When the Philistines came, he asked, shall I go up, and the Lord said go (2 Samuel 5:19). They came a second time, and David did not assume the first answer covered the second fight. He asked again, and this time God told him to circle around behind them (2 Samuel 5:23). Same enemy, different orders. David inquired every single time. Israel at Ai inquired no time. Look at the very next chapter and you see the wound again. The Gibeonites fool Israel with a costume, and the writer tells us why. They did not ask counsel from the Lord (Joshua 9:14).

Here is the design of God. He set you in the flesh, and He empowers you by the Spirit. There is no power so great that you cannot beat it in the Spirit. There is no sin so small that you can beat it in your flesh. The Jerichos rarely take us down. The Ais do, the little battle we think we can fight alone, and they hand us our worst defeat. The thing we are sure we can handle is the very thing we will never pray about. That is tomorrow's trouble.

## Application

1. Where in your life are you running a plan you drew up yourself and never once handed to God for approval?
2. Before you act on a decision you are already leaning toward, stop today and actually ask the Lord, shall I go up? Wait for His answer before you move.

## Challenge

Pick the one decision you have already made up your mind about this week. Today, take it back to God in prayer as if the answer is still open, and ask Him to confirm it or redirect it before you act.

## Prayer

Father, I have written my own orders and assumed You would sign them. I have sized up my battles and forgotten to seek You. Forgive my presumption. Teach me to inquire the way David did, every time, even against an enemy I have already beaten once. Remind me that no power is too great for Your Spirit, and no sin is too small for my flesh to lose to. Keep me from charging up the hill while the ark stays in the camp. I will not move today until I have asked You. In Jesus' name, amen.
