## Devotional

The most dangerous moment in your walk with God is not after a failure but a moment of success. You see, failure drives us to our knees. Victory tempts us to our feet. Victory entices us to stick our chest out and make us feel like we can't lose. I've been there and learned a very costly lesson.

This is where Joshua was...again. First with Jericho and now with Ai. He had just won the kind of victory that builds momentum. Ai was rubble. The road north into the heart of Canaan lay wide open, king after king, city after city, all of it within reach. All of it was ready for the taking. Yet, instead, Joshua marches the whole nation more than twenty miles in the wrong direction, up a barren slope called Mount Ebal, and he stops to build an altar (Joshua 8:30).

Scripture tells us how he builds it, "an altar of uncut stones, upon which no man has wielded an iron tool" (Joshua 8:31). No chisel. No hammer. No human craftsmanship allowed. This was the old command God gave back at Sinai (Exodus 20:25). I found it odd that God did not allow Joshua to exercise craftsmanship. Maybe it was a time issue or maybe it was a man issue. Uncut stones in scripture almost always deal with the idea that God doesn't want man involved in the process.

There is a man in all of us who wants to bring his chisel to the altar. He wants to add a little something, a clean record, a good week, a stack of religious effort laid on top of the blood as if the blood needed the help. Uncut stones say no. You cannot add to what God is doing. You cannot lend your advice or ability to something that He alone gets the glory for. The altar holds what God provides, and it turns away what we manufacture.

So watch what Joshua actually does on that mountain. He stopped to build. He stopped to sacrifice, laying burnt offerings and peace offerings on those rough stones (Joshua 8:31). He stopped to read the law out loud to people who needed reminding. The strength of that whole nation in those hours was found in stopping. Wow...how many times do we miss the strength of what God is doing because we fail to stop? (Sermon for another day.)

We are not really good at this. It feels difficult or odd or purposeless. Sunday's pause before communion may have felt wasteful to some because we're used to treating the pause like wasted time, like ground we are handing back to the enemy. The soul that never stops to remember who gave it the victory will slowly start believing it won it on its own. And people who believe they won it themselves are a people one battle away from another. Ai. The most dangerous moment in your walk with God.

Joshua stopped on the mountain of cursing to remember God before he ever fought again. But not to reflect on the victories and failures as to who they belonged to. He stopped for another reason. He stopped to make a choice and to present a choice.  His hope was for the people of Israel to choose life.

Tomorrow we stand on that same hillside, because there were two mountains in that valley, not one, and between them God set a choice no one in Israel was allowed to ignore.

## Application

1. After your last real victory, did you stop at the altar, or did you sprint straight at the next thing?
2. Build one deliberate pause into today to thank God for something He gave you, without asking Him for anything in return.

## Challenge

Before you start the work in front of you today, stop for five quiet minutes. Name one recent win out loud, hand it back to God as His gift, and resist the urge to ask Him for the next thing while you do it.

## Prayer

Lord, You know how fast I move after a win, already chasing the next thing before I have thanked You for the last one. Slow me down. Build the altar in me that bars my own tools, so I quit trying to add my work to what only Your blood can do. Make me a person who stops to remember who handed me every victory I have ever had. I lay my hammer down at Your feet today. In Jesus' name, amen.
