## Devotional

The most dangerous moment in my walk with God has never been right after I fail. It is right after I win. Failure drives me to my knees. Victory tempts me to my feet, chest out, already scanning the horizon for the next thing to conquer.

Joshua had just won the kind of victory that builds momentum. Ai was rubble. The road north into the heart of Canaan lay wide open, king after king, city after city, all of it within reach. Any general worth his salt presses an advantage like that. Instead Joshua marches the whole nation more than twenty miles in the wrong direction, up a barren slope called Mount Ebal, and he stops to build an altar (Joshua 8:30).

Look at how he builds it: "an altar of uncut stones, upon which no man has wielded an iron tool" (Joshua 8:31). No chisel. No hammer. No human craftsmanship allowed anywhere near it. This was the old command God gave back at Sinai (Exodus 20:25), and it cuts against everything in us. The one place we most want to show our work, the altar, the very spot where we get right with God, is the one place He bars our tools at the door.

There is a man in all of us who wants to bring his chisel to the altar. He wants to add a little something, a clean record, a good week, a stack of religious effort laid on top of the blood as if the blood needed the help. The uncut stone says no. You cannot carve your way into being right with God. You cannot sand the rock smooth until it earns the sacrifice. The altar holds what God provides, and it turns away what we manufacture.

So watch what Joshua actually does on that mountain. He stopped to build. He stopped to sacrifice, laying burnt offerings and peace offerings on those rough stones (Joshua 8:31). He stopped to read the law out loud to people who needed reminding. The strength of that whole nation in that hour was found in stopping.

We are not good at this. We treat the pause like wasted time, like ground we are handing back to the enemy. The soul that never stops to remember who gave it the victory will slowly start believing it won the thing on its own. A people convinced they won it themselves are a people one battle away from another Ai.

Joshua stopped on the mountain of cursing to remember God before he ever fought again. Tomorrow we stand on that same hillside, because there were two mountains in that valley, not one, and between them God set a choice no one in Israel was allowed to dodge.

## Application

1. After your last real victory, did you stop at the altar, or did you sprint straight at the next thing?
2. Build one deliberate pause into today to thank God for something He gave you, without asking Him for anything in return.

## Challenge

Before you start the work in front of you today, stop for five quiet minutes. Name one recent win out loud, hand it back to God as His gift, and resist the urge to ask Him for the next thing while you do it.

## Prayer

Lord, You know how fast I move after a win, already chasing the next thing before I have thanked You for the last one. Slow me down. Build the altar in me that bars my own tools, so I quit trying to add my work to what only Your blood can do. Make me a person who stops to remember who handed me every victory I have ever had. I lay my hammer down at Your feet today. In Jesus' name, amen.
