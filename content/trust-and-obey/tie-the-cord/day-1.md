## Devotional

Have you ever felt like every step you take this week has been triggering a trap? Like God has you in a room full of mousetraps and every step says gotcha. I want you to know that is actually a good place to be. The surgical hands of God are still hands of God. And this week, he has a question he wants to put in your lap.

Here it is. When you hope in God for your marriage to be healed, does that hope look any different from the hope of your coworker who does not believe a single thing you believe? When you pray for your body to be healed, is there something about the way you carry that hope that separates it from how the unbeliever carries theirs? Because the unbeliever wants the marriage whole too. The unbeliever wants the body healed and the prodigal home and the bills paid and the diagnosis reversed, just like we do. So if we both want the same things and both hope for the same things, what exactly makes our faith different? Why does my hope in God look identical to the hope of someone who does not believe what I claim to believe?

Joshua 2 answers that question through a woman most of us do not expect. Her name is Rahab. She is a Gentile prostitute living inside the walls of Jericho, a fortress city with double walls that the ancient world had never seen the equal of, and she is about to show us more about faith than most of us have learned in years of church attendance.

Here is the scene. Joshua sends two spies into Jericho before the nation crosses the Jordan. They end up at Rahab's house, and they are identified almost immediately. The king of Jericho has men at her door within the same night. Everyone inside those walls has heard the same report by now. The Red Sea. The total destruction of Sihon and Og, the kings east of the Jordan. The testimony of God's power has reached every house in that city. And everyone in Jericho is going to respond to it. But not everyone is going to respond the same way.

In verse 9, Rahab looks at these two strangers, men who serve a God she has only heard about, and she says, "I know that the Lord has given you the land." Not, I've heard. Not, I think so. Not, the rumor going around is. She says I know. She's making a confession. And it is the first sign of a faith that is actually going somewhere the rest of that city's residents failed to go.

The same testimony landed on Rahab that landed on the king of Jericho and every citizen inside those walls. What she did with it was different. Tomorrow, we are going to look at exactly how different, and ask which response looks most like ours.

## Application

1. When you compare your hope in God right now to the hope of someone who does not believe, what does the difference actually look like? Where is it visible?

2. Rahab said "I know" rather than "I hope so." In the area you are most urgently trusting God right now, which of those two words better describes what you actually believe?

## Challenge

Sit with the opening question today. Not to feel guilty about it but to answer it honestly. Pick one area where you are hoping God will come through, and ask yourself whether anyone watching your life from the outside could tell that your hope is different. If the answer is no, that is where this week starts.

## Prayer

Father, I am asking you today to expose the places where my hope in you looks no different from the hope of someone who does not believe. I do not want a hope that merely wishes. I do not want a faith that stays inside my chest and calls itself prayer. Teach me to say what Rahab said. Not I hope so. I know. Not because the proof has arrived but because you are who you say you are, and you have never failed to keep what you have promised. In Jesus' name, amen.
