## Devotional

The stones came out of the river. They got carried to a place called Gilgal. And at Gilgal, Joshua set them up.

That word matters. Set up. Stacked. Built into a structure with a location and a public character. Not a private prayer journal. Not a quiet moment of personal gratitude. A pile of rocks standing in the middle of the camp that the whole nation walks past every single day. The first Passover eaten in the promised land gets celebrated in the shadow of this pile. Gilgal becomes Israel's base of operations for the entire conquest. Samuel will judge Israel there. The stones are not sitting at the edge of camp where someone might stumble on them. They are at the center. By design.

Memorials do not whisper. Memorials do not hide. Memorials stand somewhere a child can see them, trip over them, and ask about them.

Look at verses 21 through 23. When your children ask their fathers, "What do these stones mean?" then you shall let your children know. Not the priest. Not the teacher. Not the pastor. You. The qualification God puts on the assignment is not theologian. It is witness. He does not say: when your children ask, give them a systematic answer with all the correct cross-references. He says: tell them what God did for us. Every person reading this can do that.

The transmission structure in Deuteronomy 6:6-7 follows the same logic. These words shall be on your heart, and you shall talk of them when you sit in your house, when you walk by the way, when you lie down, and when you rise. The faith does not transmit in a classroom one hour a week. It transmits in the texture of daily life. In how you respond when the diagnosis comes back bad. In what you pray when the bank account runs out. In what you stop watching when the Lord says stop. Your children are not waiting for your explanation. They are watching for your example. Every visible act of faithfulness is a rock on your shoulder. Every prayer said out loud at the dinner table is a rock on your shoulder. Every refusal to compromise when no one else was watching is a rock on your shoulder, and the pile is testifying to something bigger than your life.

Now look at where the whole passage lands. Verse 24. "So that all the peoples of the earth may know that the hand of the Lord is mighty, that you may fear the Lord your God forever."

Two purposes. The nations. And the next generation. Both of them land on the same word: fear.

Most of us have been taught that fear and love are opposites, that perfect love casts out fear and therefore the goal of the Christian life is to arrive at a place where the fear of God is gone entirely. That reading misses the text. The fear of the Lord in the Old Testament is not dread. It is not the terror of a slave who does not know if the master is going to strike. Fear is the right-sized response to a God who stops rivers, who sends His Son into the Jordan of death and comes back up, who takes the record of everything you owed and buries it under the water, and then pulls you out of the water and seats you in a position you had no right to occupy.

Fear is what makes worship worship instead of entertainment. Fear is what makes obedience obedience instead of negotiation.

A child who walks up to the pile of stones in the front yard and hears a parent say, "That is what God did for us," is being formed in the fear of the Lord without anyone having to define the phrase. The pile is doing the work. The question the child asks at the stones is the beginning of a lifetime of asking the right questions about who God is and what He has done.

Here is the call for this week. Pull one stone out of the river. One stone. One person to tell. One visible thing built into the life of your household that your children can point to and ask about. Could be a verse on the wall. Could be a story told at the dinner table this week. Could be a prayer said out loud over your kids that names specifically what God did for you. Pick the thing. Do not wait for a better moment. Today is verse 19. The stones are in your hand. Gilgal is waiting.

Do not leave the testimony in the water.

## Application

1. What would it look like in your home this week to make your faith visible rather than private, something the people closest to you could see and ask about?
2. How does the fear of the Lord, understood correctly as the right-sized response to who God is, change the way you approach obedience this week?

## Challenge

Build one visible memorial in your home this week. Write a verse on a notecard and put it somewhere your family will see it. Tell your children a specific story of what God did for you. Pray out loud at dinner and name the thing God delivered you from. One stone. One act. One deliberate, visible monument to what God has done. Then trust Him with what it produces.

## Prayer

Father, You are the God who stopped the Jordan. You are the God who sent the greater Joshua into the same river to swallow up the death we deserved. You pulled us out of the place we belonged and put us in the place only Your Son deserved. We have left too many stones in the river. We have called it humility when it was fear of something smaller than You. Forgive us. Put a stone in our hands this week. Show us the person to tell. Show us the moment to speak. Make our children fear You forever. Let the nations know that Your hand is mighty. In Jesus' name, Amen.
