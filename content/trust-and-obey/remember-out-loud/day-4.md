## Devotional

Most people read right past Joshua 4:9. Do not.

The text says Joshua set up twelve stones in the middle of the Jordan, in the place where the priests' feet had stood. Not at Gilgal. In the river. Under the returning water. Buried.

Two piles. Same number. Same location. Different fate.

Twelve stones came out of the river. Twelve stones went in and stayed.

Both piles are part of the same monument. They are testifying to the same event from two different sides of the water. The pile on the bank said: look what God did for us. The pile in the river said: something happened down here that made the crossing possible. The bank pile was seen. The river pile was buried. Both were God's idea. Neither one was supposed to exist without the other.

The river pile is pointing at something specific. Colossians 2:14 says God canceled the record of debt that stood against us, nailing it to the cross. Micah 7:19 says He casts our sins into the depths of the sea. The record of what we owed was supposed to go down and stay down. The sin goes in the river. That is God's work, and He does not dig that pile back up. Neither should you.

But the pile on the bank was supposed to come out. That pile is the testament that the exchange happened. And the exchange is bigger than most of us have let it be.

He left the place He was supposed to be, heaven, and came to the place we deserved to be, a cross. He took us from the place we deserved to be, that cross, and put us in the place only He deserved, seated in the heavenly places. Ephesians 2:6 says God raised us up with Him and seated us with Him in the heavenly places in Christ Jesus. That is a position exchange, and it is staggering on its own.

But the exchange does not stop at position. It moves to proclamation.

Jesus, in His death, was silenced. He could no longer proclaim the Kingdom from a sealed tomb. But He rose. And when He rose, He did not resume the proclamation for Himself alone. Acts 1:8 says: you will be my witnesses. Not just beneficiaries of what He did, witnesses of it. You did not only trade places with Him. You traded what you were boasting about. You traded the proclamation itself.

He died so you could live. He was silenced so you could speak. He descended into the place of judgment so you could ascend to the place of authority. The position exchange led directly to a proclamation exchange, and the two cannot be separated without losing half the gospel.

This is why the testimony stone is not optional and not humble to leave in the water. When you stay silent about what God did for you, you are not being private. You are letting the proclamation exchange stay buried. The sin was supposed to go under the water. The testimony was supposed to come up. When you confuse the two piles, you bury the wrong one. You carry the guilt out on your shoulder and leave the grace under the river.

Pull the testimony out. The sin is gone. Micah 7:19 says God does not remember it. The record has been nailed to the cross by a God who canceled it permanently. But the testimony of what He did with it belongs on the bank. It belongs somewhere your neighbor, your coworker, and your children can walk up to and ask about.

Tomorrow, that last part, the children and the next generation, is where all of this has been heading.

## Application

1. Where in your life have you been treating silence about God's work as humility, when the exchange actually commissioned you to speak?
2. What is the specific thing God pulled you out of that, when named out loud, becomes a witness to the exchange?

## Challenge

Write out the exchange for your own life in four lines. What you were. What He did. Where you are now. What He is asking you to say. That four-line testimony is a stone pile. Find one person to say it to before Sunday.

## Prayer

Father, You did not exchange my position so I could keep quiet about it. You exchanged my proclamation too. What I was boasting about before You found me, the record of everything I trusted in instead of You, You nailed to the cross. And You gave me a new thing to proclaim. Forgive me for treating the testimony like a private possession. The exchange was public. The cross was public. The empty tomb was public. Let the pile on my bank be just as visible. In Jesus' name, Amen.
