## Devotional

The crossing is done. The Jordan has closed back up. The priests are standing on dry ground. Jericho is right there on the horizon, close enough to make out the walls. Every soldier in camp is wet, exhausted, and ready to move. The conquest is sitting right in front of them.

And God interrupts everything to commission a construction project.

Twelve men. Twelve stones. Out of the riverbed. On your shoulder. Set them down tonight.

The question most of us never ask is what God requires the day after He moves. We know how to ask for the deliverance. We know how to thank Him when it comes. But Joshua 4 is the chapter nobody preaches, because it deals with what happens on the morning after the miracle. And God's first assignment is not a battle plan. It is not a victory speech. It is twelve men carrying rocks out of a river.

God stops everything for a construction project before the first battle starts because He knows something about His people that we keep forgetting about ourselves. He knows we forget. The same crowd that watched the Red Sea split in Exodus 14 was complaining about water three days into the wilderness in Exodus 17. The same crowd that ate manna from heaven was grumbling about the menu by Numbers 11. God knows that what He did in that river last week is not going to preach itself in your living room next week unless somebody puts the stones on their shoulder right now.

Deuteronomy 8 gives you the diagnosis straight. Moses told them: when the houses are good, when the flocks are large, when the bread is plentiful, you will forget the Lord your God. The danger is not hardship. The danger is prosperity. And Deuteronomy 8 is the backstory God is working against in Joshua 4. The monument at Gilgal is a preemptive strike against the forgetting that comfort produces.

So the construction project is not a delay of the conquest. Hear me on this. The construction project is the conquest of the next generation. Before Israel takes Jericho, God makes them take their children's future. The pile of stones comes before the battle because without the pile, the battle cannot mean anything to anyone born after it. Psalm 78:4 says the generation that experienced God's deliverance was responsible to "tell to the coming generation the glorious deeds of the Lord, and his might, and the wonders that he has done." That transmission does not happen by accident. It requires someone to carry something heavy out of the river and stack it somewhere visible.

The Hebrew verb zakar means to remember, but not the way we use the word. In the Old Testament, zakar is active and consequential. When God "remembers" in Genesis 8:1, He acts on Noah's behalf. When Israel is commanded to zakar, the command always carries a behavioral expectation attached to it. The opposite of zakar is not neutral forgetfulness. It is the kind of forgetting that led the wilderness generation directly into covenant failure. God builds the monument because He knows what forgetting costs.

What has God done in your life that you have never carried out of the river? Do not answer that fast. Tomorrow we are going to find out that the answer was never supposed to stay in your head.

## Application

1. What specific act of God in your life have you kept private that was never meant to be private?
2. Where in your daily routine does God's past faithfulness go unmentioned, and what would it take to change that this week?

## Challenge

Write down one specific thing God has done for you in the last three years. Not a category like "He has been faithful." A specific event, a specific answer to prayer, a specific moment when He moved. Write the date if you remember it. Write the name of the person it involved. That written record is the first stone out of the river.

## Prayer

Father, I confess that I have kept too many of Your acts buried in my own memory, as though they belonged to me. You stopped rivers. You answered prayers I had almost stopped praying. You delivered me from things I deserved to stay in. Forgive me for treating Your faithfulness like a private journal entry. Give me the courage to carry the stone out this week. In Jesus' name, Amen.
