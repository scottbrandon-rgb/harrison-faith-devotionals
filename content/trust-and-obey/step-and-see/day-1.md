## Devotional

Joshua rose early. He set out from Shittim, came to the Jordan, and lodged there with all Israel before they passed over. Three days of preparation before the first command went out. And when the command came, it was simple: follow the ark when you see it move, keep your distance, and go where it leads. What comes after that is where the story gets complicated.

Read the parenthetical note buried inside verse fifteen. "Now the Jordan overflows all its banks throughout the time of harvest." One sentence that rewrites the whole scene. God had forty years to bring Israel to this crossing. Forty. He could have brought two million people to the Jordan in September when the water runs low. He could have brought them in the dry season. He brought them at flood stage.

The Jordan River in its normal season is not much of an obstacle. Depending on where you cross, you can find a shallow stretch. But when the snowmelt comes down from Mount Hermon in the north, the river swells. Fast, cold, and at flood stage wide enough to spread half a mile across the valley. Julie and I took the kids to Colorado a few years ago and showed up expecting a calm August float on the Arkansas River. They had fires burning outside the outfitter in the middle of summer. I did not understand that until we hit the water. Snowmelt does not warm up. It runs fast and cold all year long. That is what Israel was standing in front of.

Hear me. God did not bring them here in spite of the flood stage. He brought them here because of it. Forty years of planning and He chose this moment. What looks like the worst possible timing is exactly His timing. He does not need better conditions to do what He promised.

There is a version of faith that wants calm water. We want the river down, the current slow, the crossing manageable, and then we will step out. And what God is communicating through this entire chapter is that He is not waiting for manageable. He is going to use the flood stage to show something that could never be seen in calm water: that He is the one doing the crossing.

This is the pattern all through Scripture. The fiery furnace in Daniel 3. The lion's den. The Red Sea. When Nebuchadnezzar threw the three Hebrew children into the fire, he was completing God's plan. The same king who threw them in would later write with his own hand, "How great are his signs, how mighty his wonders!" That does not happen if God delivers them before the fire. The fire was the plan.

Your flood stage is not a mistake. God is not scrambling to respond to the timing of your problem. He chose this moment. He has a reason for this particular crossing, and that reason is revelation. He is about to make Himself known, and He does not make Himself known on the easy crossings.

Tomorrow, before a single foot touches that water, Joshua is going to say something Israel needed to hear. And you need to hear it too.

## Application

1. Looking at your current flood stage, what would it mean to believe that God chose this timing intentionally rather than arrived at it by accident?
2. Think of a past moment that looked like the worst possible timing but proved to be God's plan. What did that crossing reveal about His character that calm water never would have?

## Challenge

Name the flood stage you are in right now. Write it down plainly. Then write this beside it: "God chose this moment." Carry that with you today and let it do its work on the fear underneath your situation.

## Prayer

Father, I confess that I want calm water. I want the conditions right before I trust You with the crossing. Forgive me for reading the flood stage as Your absence when it is Your plan. You chose this moment for Israel and You chose this moment for me. Help me to receive it as what it is. You are not scrambling. You are not late. You are already working upstream from everything I am afraid of. Give me the eyes to see this problem as Your plan, and hold me steady until the crossing is finished. In Jesus' name, amen.
